2.  Products in ScopeThe Shared ArchitectureTicket and Neat are two distinct products aimed at two distinct industry segments, but they run on a single shared backend. Both are powered by the Vatic competitive intelligence engine — the same data pipeline, the same API stack, the same infrastructure. They are differentiated by an industry_type flag in the database, by their respective brand identities, and by the competitive signals that matter in each industry.This architecture has a direct implication for how costs work: every fixed subscription the business pays — Data365, Firecrawl, Supabase, Vercel — is amortized across the combined workspace count of both products. A Ticket customer and a Neat customer both contribute to diluting the fixed cost base. That shared amortization is one of the strongest financial arguments for pursuing both verticals in parallel.TicketCompetitive intelligence built for restaurant operators. Ticket monitors competitor pricing, menu changes, promotional activity, local SEO movements, social signals, and customer review patterns — and delivers them in the form of daily briefings timed to how restaurants actually work. It is not a dashboard. It is not a reporting tool. It is an intelligence brief delivered before the window to act closes.• ICP: Restaurant owners, operators, and marketing leads — single locations through 50+ location brands• Domain: getticket.ai• Stage: Early access• Voice: The Expeditor — sharp, fast, kitchen-confidentNeatCompetitive intelligence built for liquor store operators. Neat monitors competitor pricing on spirits and beer, selection and inventory signals, new store activity, customer sentiment, local search visibility, and event-driven promotional activity. It delivers intelligence that is already distilled — patient, verified, grounded in the business of moving bottles.• ICP: Liquor store owners and operators — independents through regional chains• Domain: goneat.ai• Stage: Early access• Voice: The Insider — knowledgeable, curated, unhurriedVatic is the technology layer. Ticket and Neat are the products operators interact with. Do not position Vatic as a standalone consumer product or describe either product as 'Vatic for restaurants' or 'Vatic for liquor stores.'

5.  What Makes Up COGSCOGS (Cost of Goods Sold) is the direct cost of delivering the product to a paying customer. It does not include salaries, marketing spend, or general overhead — those are operating expenses. For Ticket and Neat, COGS is entirely made up of the API and infrastructure costs required to run the intelligence pipeline for each workspace.The cost stack has two distinct types: fixed subscriptions (charged the same regardless of how many workspaces exist) and variable costs (charged per workspace per month). Understanding the difference is the key to reading the break-even analysis.Fixed Costs — Shared Across All WorkspacesThese costs are incurred once per month regardless of whether you have one customer or one hundred. They dilute rapidly as workspace count grows.SERVICEMONTHLY COSTWHAT IT DOES / ECONOMY OF SCALE NOTEData365€300–€850 + / moProvides Instagram, Facebook, and TikTok profile and post data. 6.  What Changed Since the March 2026 ModelThe original Vatic pricing strategy document, prepared by Anand Iyer in March 2026, was a well-constructed analysis. The tier structure, vendor selection, and competitive benchmarking all remain valid. However, three inputs to the COGS model require correction. These corrections were identified through direct research against live vendor pricing pages in April 2026.1.  The Google Places $200/Month Free Credit Expired?  CRITICAL: The Google Maps Platform $200/month free credit that the original model relied upon expired on February 28, 2025. Every workspace has been incurring real Google Places costs since March 2025.The original model assumed Google Places would be $0 for the first four to five workspaces because the $200 monthly credit covered that volume. This was correct when the model was written — but the credit expired fourteen months ago.Google replaced the blanket credit with per-SKU free usage caps. The relevant caps are small: 1,000 free Place Details Photo events per month and 10,000 free Place Details Essentials events per month. At typical usage with photo hash deduplication applied, a single Operator-tier workspace consumes approximately 1,620 photo events per month. The free cap runs out well before the first paying customer.The financial impact: the original model showed the first four workspaces running at zero Google Places cost. The corrected model shows every workspace paying Places costs from day one, with the full per-workspace impact running approximately $11–21/month depending on tier. This is the primary reason the Entry tier shows lower margins at low scale than the original model projected.SCALE POINTORIGINAL MODEL (with $200 credit)CORRECTED MODEL (no credit)1–4 workspacesGoogle Places = $0/wsGoogle Places = $10–21/ws5–10 workspacesGoogle Places = $10–44/wsGoogle Places = $10–21/ws (same, now from ws 1)30 workspacesGoogle Places = ~$37/wsGoogle Places = ~$20/ws (dedup optimized)Volume discountNo threshold-based discount$5.60/1K photos at ~62 Operator workspaces2.  Vercel Was Significantly UnderestimatedThe original model allocated $0–2 per workspace per month for Vercel across all scale points. This was incorrect.Vercel Pro costs $20 per deploying seat per month. With three deploying team members, that is a fixed $60 per month regardless of workspace count. In September 2025, Vercel restructured its Pro plan from resource-allocation-based billing to a credit-based model — but the per-seat cost remained the same.The impact is most significant at low subscriber counts. At one workspace, the Vercel cost alone is $60/workspace. At five workspaces it is $12. At thirty workspaces it is $2 — which aligns with where the original model placed it. Vercel's cost profile is a fixed floor that amortizes linearly and becomes negligible above twenty to thirty workspaces.3.  Resend Was Missing from COGS EntirelyResend is the transactional email platform that delivers daily intelligence briefings to customers. It was in the product stack but was not included in the original COGS model.The impact is small but real: Resend's free tier covers 3,000 emails per month, which accommodates up to 100 workspaces (at 30 emails per workspace per month for daily briefings). Beyond 100 workspaces, the Pro plan adds $20/month. This is not a material margin risk, but the line item belongs in COGS since it is a direct cost of delivering the product to a paying customer.The corrected model is more conservative at low scale and more accurate at all scale points. The fundamental pricing thesis is unchanged: three tiers at $149/$299/$499 is the right structure. The corrections sharpen the picture of when each tier reaches healthy SaaS margin territory.

At that point, total credit consumption crosses the Hobby plan limit and the product moves to the Standard plan at $83/month. Both are fixed monthly costs ($60 and $25 respectively) that divide evenly across an increasing workspace count. Neither has step functions. Their contribution to margin improvement is steady and predictable throughout the growth curve.4. Above that point, photo metadata calls drop from $7.00/1K to $5.60/1K — a 20% cost reduction on the third-largest cost component. Both are purely variable and scale linearly with each workspace. DataForSEO has no automatic volume discounts below tens of millions of events per month. Gemini is token-priced with stable rates. The only levers here are cadence management (Entry and mid tiers on weekly rather than daily SEO refresh) and context caching for repeated prompts (up to 90% savings on cache reads).

10.  Engineering Dependencies — Must Ship Before LaunchThree engineering optimizations are required before any customer is charged. They are not enhancement features. They are cost-control mechanisms that make the Entry tier viable and improve margins across all tiers. Without them, the Well and Table tiers run at approximately 23% gross margin — not viable for a subscription business.?  All three items below must ship before any paid customer goes live. They are not optional and they are not polish. They are margin prerequisites.1.  Photo Hash Deduplication — VikramWhat it does: Before fetching photo metadata from Google Places, compare the file hash of each photo against the stored hash from the previous fetch. If unchanged, skip the fetch entirely.Why it matters: Google Places charges $7 per 1,000 photo metadata calls. Without deduplication, every workspace fetches up to 5,400 photo calls per month (6 entities ? 30 photos ? 30 daily checks). With 70% deduplication — a reasonable expectation since most competitor photos do not change day to day — this drops to approximately 1,620 calls per month. That is a 70% reduction in the single largest per-workspace variable cost.Impact on Entry tier margin: Without dedup, Entry tier COGS runs approximately $120–130/workspace at launch. With dedup, it falls to $55–70/workspace — the difference between a loss-making tier and a viable acquisition product.• Owner: Vikram Sethi• Priority: Launch blocker — must ship before first paid customer• Dependency: None — this is a pure data pipeline optimization2.  Social Platform Tiering — VikramWhat it does: Configure the data pipeline so that Entry-tier (Well/Table) workspaces only consume Instagram credits from Data365, triggering the single-network €300/month plan rather than the three-network €600/month plan.Why it matters: Data365 is the largest fixed cost in the model. A workspace that only monitors Instagram should not pay for Facebook and TikTok credit consumption. Gating Entry-tier workspaces to Instagram only reduces the Data365 allocation for those workspaces by half — from $33/workspace to $16.50/workspace at thirty workspaces.Prerequisite: Confirm with Data365 (Anastasiya) that the €300 single-network plan supports the expected credit volume for Entry-tier usage before this ships. The credit math has been modeled but should be validated against the actual plan terms.• Owner: Vikram Sethi• Priority: Launch blocker — must ship before first paid customer• Dependency: Confirm Data365 plan credit capacity with Anastasiya3.  SEO Cadence Tiering — VikramWhat it does: Configure DataForSEO refresh frequency by tier — weekly for Entry and Mid tiers, twice weekly for the Top tier.Why it matters: DataForSEO is a pure pay-as-you-go cost at approximately $4.95 per refresh. Weekly cadence (four refreshes per month) costs $19.80/workspace. Daily cadence would cost $138.60/workspace — prohibitive for the Entry tier. SEO signals move slowly enough that weekly cadence is appropriate for most operator use cases, with the Top tier justifying twice-weekly due to its larger competitive set.• Owner: Vikram Sethi• Priority: Launch blocker — must ship before first paid customer• Dependency: None — this is a configuration variable per workspace
11.  Vendor Negotiation PrioritiesThree vendor relationships have identifiable negotiation leverage at specific scale points. These are not urgent at launch but should be tracked and acted on as workspace count grows.Data365 — Negotiate Custom Contract at 50+ WorkspacesData365 does not publish pricing publicly — it is a sales-negotiated relationship. The €600/month three-network plan covers up to approximately thirty workspaces before the next plan tier is required. At fifty workspaces, a custom volume contract should produce meaningful per-workspace cost reduction relative to the step-function pricing model.The leverage argument is straightforward: two products on the same Data365 account means the combined workspace count grows faster than either product alone. At fifty combined workspaces, you represent a meaningful and predictable revenue relationship for Data365. Initiate the negotiation conversation before you need the new plan — not after you hit the threshold.Google Maps Platform — Self-Service Volume Discounts Apply AutomaticallyGoogle Places volume discounts for the Photos SKU are automatic and require no negotiation: $7.00/1K below 100,000 events, $5.60/1K between 100K and 500K, $4.20/1K above 500K. These thresholds are reached at approximately 62, 309, and 617 Operator/Call/Shift-tier workspaces respectively. No action required — the discounts apply automatically to the billing account.At 10,000,000+ monthly billable events, Google Maps offers custom-negotiated pricing. This threshold corresponds to approximately 6,170 workspaces — not a near-term concern, but worth knowing exists.DataForSEO — Contact Sales at High VolumeDataForSEO is pure pay-as-you-go with a $50 minimum deposit and credits that never expire. The company offers custom pricing for large-volume customers. At current scale projections, DataForSEO costs run linearly without discount. At approximately one hundred workspaces and above, a volume conversation is worth initiating. The strongest lever available today is the already-modeled weekly cadence for entry and mid tiers — which reduces per-workspace DataForSEO cost by half relative to daily cadence.
